AI in Employment

Ethics, Sociology, and Governance of Artificial Intelligence

Group 3 | MAIB AI 219

Why AI in Employment Matters

  • Automated screening tools are now widely used across hiring pipelines
  • Screening algorithms function as gatekeepers — they determine who gets seen, not just who gets hired
  • Upstream exclusion is invisible: rejected candidates never learn why, never get to appeal
  • At scale, a small bias compounds into thousands of lost opportunities per year

The Core Problem

Those filtered out never reach the hiring manager

Evidence base: EEOC guidance (2023), EU AI Act discussions, investigative reporting on algorithmic hiring — see references.

System Framing

  • What hiring AI does: Ranks candidates by predicted "fit" — determining visibility, not merit
  • HireVue-style systems analyze video interviews, speech patterns, and behavioral signals to score candidates before human review
  • EU AI Act classifies employment AI as high-risk — requiring audits, documentation, and human oversight

FairJob as Proxy: A real-world job recommendation dataset (1.07M records) that mirrors HireVue's gatekeeping function — who gets recommended, who doesn't.

Why Aggregate Fairness Misleads

  • "Passing" metrics can hide localized harm — overall DIR masks segment-level disparities
  • Non-senior bias is 17% worse than senior positions — exactly where opportunity equity matters most
  • Legal compliance (4/5ths rule) is a floor, not a ceiling
Intersectional analysis reveals what aggregate metrics obscure.

Ethics Lens

Consequentialism

8.5% disadvantage translates to measurable harm: thousands of lost opportunities annually. Mitigation is feasible — choosing not to act maximizes harm.

Deontology

Candidates have a right to equal consideration. Opaque features and unexplained rejections treat humans as data points, not autonomous agents deserving explanation.

Virtue Ethics

What kind of organization deploys biased screening? Justice, transparency, and accountability are compromised when efficiency trumps fairness.

Sociology Lens

  • Algorithmic gatekeeping: AI decides who is seen — not human judgment
  • Power asymmetry: Platforms have full visibility; candidates have none
  • Opacity creates helplessness: Rejection without explanation prevents adaptation

Who Benefits

Platforms, Employers, Privileged Groups

Who Is Harmed

Unprivileged Groups, Entry-Level Applicants, Those with Disabilities

Governance Lens

Title VII (US)

Disparate impact doctrine applies to AI. 4/5ths rule sets minimum threshold. Employers liable even for vendor algorithms (Mobley v. Workday).

ADA (US)

HireVue's video analysis disadvantages deaf and disabled applicants. ACLU filed formal complaint (March 2025). Accommodations required before assessment.

EU AI Act

Employment AI = high-risk system. Requires bias audits, documentation, human oversight, and activity logging. Effective 2026-2027.

Compliance is a minimum standard — it is not ethical adequacy.

Mitigation Is Feasible

  • Reweighing: Adjust sample weights to correct historical bias → DIR = 1.01
  • Threshold Adjustment: Group-specific thresholds achieve perfect parity → DIR = 1.00
  • No major performance loss: ROC-AUC maintained at ~77%

The Accountability Shift

Once mitigation is technically feasible

Inaction becomes a choice

  • The ethical burden shifts to organizations — ignorance is no longer a defense
  • Governance implication: "can" implies "must" — capability creates obligation
  • The question is no longer whether fair AI hiring is possible — it is. The question is whether organizations choose to implement it.

Conclusions

  • AI hiring systems exhibit measurable bias — 8.5% disadvantage for unprivileged groups, amplified at entry level
  • Aggregate compliance hides localized harm — intersectional analysis is essential
  • Mitigation works — threshold adjustment achieves near-perfect parity without sacrificing performance
  • Policy implication: Mandate pre-deployment audits, intersectional reporting, and human oversight for all employment AI
